# Early-Phase Design Rules

## Purpose

Use this file when the answer needs more than a high-level statement and must explain how the early clinical package should be designed.

This file is especially important when the user asks:

- 早期研究先做健康人还是患者
- 是否需要先健康人再做患者
- SAD 和 MAD 应该如何设计
- 是否需要患者 PK、MUsT、局部耐受和早期药效信号整合

## Core Rule

Do not answer early-phase questions with only “建议开展 I 期研究” or “建议做 SAD/MAD”.

At minimum, explain:

- why the early package exists
- who should enter first
- what uncertainty each study is trying to remove
- what signal would allow progression

## Population Choice Logic

### 1. Prefer healthy volunteers first when

- the product is not expected to cause meaningful systemic exposure beyond manageable uncertainty
- local tolerability risk is acceptable in intact skin
- disease-state skin changes are not expected to radically alter PK or tolerability
- the main early question is clean human PK, general safety, or broad dose escalation control

Typical output wording:

- “建议先在健康受试者中建立单次和短期重复给药的安全耐受与系统 PK 基线，再尽早转入患者确认病损皮肤条件下的暴露和局部耐受。”

### 2. Prefer patient-first when

- diseased skin is likely to materially change absorption or local tolerability
- the key pharmacodynamic or clinical signal can only be read in patients
- treatment area, lesion severity, or barrier disruption makes healthy-volunteer data less decision-relevant
- ethical or practical considerations make intact-skin exposure less informative than patient exposure

Typical output wording:

- “建议直接以患者为早期研究主体，因为病损皮肤状态对局部递送和系统暴露的影响是当前最关键的不确定性。”

### 3. Prefer a sequential healthy-volunteer to patient strategy when

- systemic exposure is still uncertain, but not high enough to force patient-first
- the team needs clean first-in-human PK and tolerability data before exposing patients repeatedly
- patient skin condition may still alter exposure enough that healthy-volunteer data alone are not sufficient

Typical output wording:

- “建议采用先健康受试者、后患者的两步策略：第一步在健康受试者中降低首人体暴露不确定性，第二步在患者中确认病损皮肤条件下的暴露、局部耐受和初步信号。”

## SAD Logic for Topical Innovative or Improved Programs

### Purpose

SAD is usually used to answer:

- single-application local safety and tolerability
- first systemic PK signal
- dose or exposure proportionality trend
- whether higher concentration, larger area, or more intensive application is reasonable

### Topical-Specific Design Dimensions

For topical products, “dose escalation” may involve one or more of:

- strength or concentration escalation
- application amount escalation
- treated surface-area escalation
- occluded versus non-occluded use, if clinically relevant
- drying-time, film-load, or retained-residue considerations for film-forming products

Do not assume SAD only means increasing milligram strength in the classical oral-drug sense.

### Strategy-Level SAD Design Elements To State

When recommending SAD, the answer should usually specify:

- study type: randomized, blinded, placebo or vehicle controlled when practical
- cohort logic: sequential cohorts; sentinel dosing when first-human uncertainty is material
- escalation dimension: concentration, area, amount, or a combination
- main assessments:
  - TEAEs
  - local irritation or tolerability scoring
  - systemic PK
  - local PK or biomarker sampling if feasible
- escalation decision rule:
  - acceptable local tolerability
  - no clinically meaningful systemic safety signal
  - exposure remains within a supportable range

### When SAD May Be De-Emphasized

SAD can be deemphasized or simplified when:

- the product is a lower-risk optimization of a well-known topical active
- the main risk is not first-dose systemic uncertainty but chronic local tolerability or long-term use pattern
- an integrated early patient study can answer the same question more directly

## MAD Logic for Topical Programs

### Purpose

MAD is usually used to answer:

- repeat-dose local tolerability
- systemic accumulation or time-dependent PK
- whether intended clinical frequency is tolerable and pharmacologically sensible
- whether repeated application changes local or systemic exposure

### Healthy-Volunteer MAD Is More Useful When

- the main question is repeat-dose safety and PK under intact-skin conditions
- disease-state skin is not expected to radically alter exposure
- the product has low early efficacy-readout value in healthy volunteers but still needs repeat-dose safety characterization

### Patient MAD or Early Repeat-Dose Patient PK Is More Useful When

- barrier disruption, inflammation, or lesion burden may alter exposure
- local efficacy signal and local tolerability should be assessed together
- the eventual regimen depends on patient-use behavior and diseased-skin exposure

### Strategy-Level MAD Design Elements To State

When recommending MAD, the answer should usually specify:

- repeat-dose duration aligned with the intended regimen question
  - short repeat-dose tolerance
  - early proof-of-pharmacology
  - bridge to proof-of-concept
- cohort escalation logic and stopping rules
- whether the study remains in healthy volunteers or transitions to patients
- accumulation and trough-to-peak PK assessment
- repeat-dose local tolerability monitoring
- whether exploratory biomarkers or early efficacy variables are collected

## Patient PK and MUsT Logic

### Add patient PK early when

- diseased skin may alter absorption
- healthy-volunteer PK would not be enough to justify later patient dosing
- a proof-of-concept study would otherwise be under-informed on exposure
- the dosage form may create local retention or depot behavior that only becomes meaningful after repeated patient use

### Add max-use PK or MUsT logic early when

- large treatment areas are plausible
- chronic or widespread use is intended
- systemic safety is a live risk, even for a locally acting product
- FDA strategy is in scope and exposure-control arguments matter
- film-forming or slow-release topical behavior may increase concern about cumulative exposure under real-use conditions

## Conservative Early Package Pattern

Typical conservative pattern:

1. healthy-volunteer single-application tolerability and PK, if ethically and scientifically appropriate
2. repeat-dose HV or limited patient PK and tolerability
3. patient PK or max-use PK under more realistic lesion-area conditions
4. exploratory patient efficacy or proof-of-concept study

Use this when:

- novel mechanism
- uncertain exposure
- weak strength rationale
- chronic-use ambition

## Aggressive Early Package Pattern

Typical aggressive pattern:

1. small healthy-volunteer sentinel or abbreviated PK/tolerability step, or direct patient entry if justified
2. integrated patient repeat-dose PK plus early signal study
3. faster transition into dose-confirming or pivotal-like exploration

Use this only when:

- local-delivery rationale is strong
- systemic uncertainty is limited or well managed
- the disease model allows early readout
- the team is willing to accept higher regulatory-discussion burden

## Output Rule

If the user asks for early-study design detail, the final answer should name:

- healthy volunteers, patients, or sequential entry
- whether SAD is recommended and why
- whether MAD is recommended and why
- whether patient PK or MUsT should sit before or inside proof-of-concept work
- what would trigger progression to the next stage

If the product is film-forming, also name:

- whether single-dose PK is likely to understate repeat-dose accumulation risk
- whether drying behavior, transfer risk, or residue persistence needs to be checked in early patient studies
